## Executive Summary

Operation Epic Fury opened on February 28, 2026 with concurrent US and Israeli
strikes against Iran's three principal enrichment facilities at Natanz, Fordow,
and Isfahan, marking the first direct, sustained military confrontation between
Washington and Tehran since the 1979 revolution. US Air Force B-2 sorties delivered
GBU-57 penetrators against Fordow's hardened galleries while Israeli F-35I and
F-15I formations struck Natanz centrifuge halls and Isfahan's conversion facility
across a compressed six-hour window. Iran's retaliation came within hours: a
large ballistic-missile barrage against Tel Aviv and Haifa, activation of Hezbollah's
rocket inventory, and simultaneous Houthi movement against Red Sea shipping. By
the late evening Iran had suspended all Strait of Hormuz traffic pending damage
assessment, a step that sent Brent crude up sharply in Asian trading. Preliminary
and contested casualty figures place Israeli losses in the low hundreds killed
and hundreds wounded, with Iranian losses significantly higher; US forces took
no reported fatalities. No mediation track exists. Analytical judgment: with
retaliatory capacity on both sides essentially intact and interceptor stocks
already drawing down visibly, the 72-hour window carries extreme escalation
risk and a negligible near-term ceasefire probability.

## Escalation Gauge

The opening day established the war's initial geometry in stark form. Israel and
the United States moved in tight coordination, with CENTCOM assets providing
suppression, ISR, and tanker support while Israel struck the near-border Iranian
targets and the US took the deepest and hardest sites. Iran's counter followed
the long-published Tehran playbook: an immediate, heavy ballistic volley against
Israeli urban centers, proxy activation across the Levant and the Red Sea, and
direct pressure on global energy flows through Hormuz. No meaningful diplomatic
buffer exists: Oman and Qatar signaled willingness to mediate, but Tehran rejected
any precondition that included cessation of enrichment activity. Washington
framed the operation narrowly as a counter-proliferation strike, not regime
change, which leaves rhetorical room for a later off-ramp but does not create
one. Jerusalem's framing is broader and explicitly existential, which closes
the same rhetorical room from the other side. Analytical judgment: the war
opens with every pressure dial maximized on both sides, the six analytical
clocks spread across three quadrants, and a near-zero probability of a
negotiated pause before interceptor depletion or Iranian infrastructure
collapse forces one party's hand.

The Day 1 picture also previews the second-order pressures that will shape
the coming week. Global oil futures opened Asian trading sharply higher on
the Hormuz suspension, and Asian LNG cargoes began rerouting within hours;
Taiwan's energy ministry issued a reserve-status update without committing
to any specific action. Chinese state media responded with cautious language
calling for "maximum restraint from all sides" — the boilerplate that precedes
more substantive Beijing moves. Gulf capitals, which have spent the last
several years hedging between Washington and Tehran, began issuing carefully
neutral statements while quietly raising domestic alert levels. The UN Security
Council scheduled an emergency session for the following day but without
agreed-on language. Analytical judgment: the next 72 hours will be shaped
less by new kinetic events than by how fast the energy and diplomatic
second-order pressures load onto the two principals.

## Strategic Implications

### 1. **The opening salvo establishes the war's analytical clocks**

Day 1 set every multi-clock state simultaneously, and the pattern matters more than
any single dial. Political will on both sides registers as high and stable: there
is no domestic audience in Washington, Tehran, or Jerusalem demanding a pause at
hour zero of a war whose casus belli is an active enrichment program and a series
of escalation steps the region has been watching for months. Active deadlines
are at none: no ultimatum is outstanding, no IAEA inspection window is ticking.
Energy infrastructure is degraded and worsening, driven by the Hormuz suspension
and uncertainty over whether Iranian refinery targeting will follow. Interceptor
capacity is high but already draining, a condition that will drive Israeli
tolerance for sustained ballistic barrages more than any political calculation.
Negotiation capacity is low and stable — Oman and Qatar are available but
Tehran has not accepted any terms that avoid the enrichment question. Oil reserves
are ample but will compress fast if Hormuz closure extends past 72 hours.
Analytical judgment: the war does not need a new trigger to escalate; it needs
only time.

### 2. **Hormuz closure begins the energy-MAD calculus**

The Strait of Hormuz suspension is the single most consequential non-kinetic
development of Day 1, and its mechanics will dominate the coming week. Roughly
a fifth of global oil and a quarter of liquefied natural gas crosses the strait.
A transient closure is priced in; a sustained closure is not. Two second-order
threads activate immediately. First, Taiwan's grid depends on LNG for roughly
half of its baseload, and Hormuz-routed cargoes make up a meaningful fraction
of that supply. Any disruption that extends beyond Taiwan's reserve window
creates a direct, observable link between Iranian action and Indo-Pacific
semiconductor output. Second, Chinese oil imports through Hormuz are large
enough that sustained closure forces a Beijing reaction — either public
mediation pressure or, more quietly, direct political communications to
Tehran. Analytical judgment: the energy channel is the fastest path to a
ceasefire if Tehran overreaches, and the fastest path to escalation if
Washington responds to a prolonged closure with direct strikes on Iranian
naval assets.

### 3. **Interceptor depletion is the hidden clock**

Israel's Arrow-3, Arrow-2, and David's Sling inventories are finite, and US
replenishment cycles are measured in months, not days. The first Iranian
barrage revealed engagement rates that, if sustained, will compress Israeli
magazine depth below warning thresholds inside of two weeks. Analytical
judgment: the most important single number to track in the coming briefs
is not daily casualty totals but the implied burn rate per barrage, back-solved
from intercept percentages and observed impacts. Once that number collides
with the production-replenishment number, Israeli political tolerance for
additional direct Iranian barrages will drop sharply, forcing either an
escalation against Iranian launchers at source or an unplanned pause for
resupply. Neither option is stable.
